01 · The Claim

Why "first" is literal, not marketing

A language model is four things: parameters that hold what it knows, a forward pass that turns input into output, a training loop that improves it, and an eval that tells it what "better" means. Everything calling itself a smart city has, at best, the second — sensors feeding dashboards, inference without learning. SŪRYA-1 is the first built thing on Earth to have all four. Its parameters are acres. Soil carbon, battery chemistry, herd health, orchard age — the state of the land is the state of the model, and improving the land is, literally, updating the weights.

Its forward pass is the day. Sunlight enters the solar field; the farms embed earth into tokens of food; the commons attend everyone to everyone; the labs transform; the living rings hold two thousand humans in context; and the day's single output token — time, returned to its people — is emitted at dusk. Its training loop is the night. On stored sunlight the eight subsystem models retrain on what the land actually did that day: every harvest weighed against prediction, every flight logged, every diagnosis confirmed or corrected. The town dreams in gradient descent and wakes measurably wiser. And its eval is the only honest one ever written: the flourishing of the beings inside it — measured in returned hours, health baselines, and whether the dogs run to greet you.

Farming is the model. Solar is the model. The EV loop is the model. Health is the model. They are not services bolted to a township; they are layers of one architecture, sharing one residual stream, trained by one nightly loop, governed by one frozen spine. That has never existed. This document is its release.

Definition, for the record — a Living Foundation Model: (1) its parameters are physical; (2) it trains nightly on its own telemetry; (3) its eval is the wellbeing of its inhabitants; (4) its outputs are survival goods and human time. SŪRYA-1 is the first system to satisfy all four.

04 · SūryaOS

Eight subsystem models, one nightly loop

Each is a domain foundation model fine-tuned from open weights on the township's own telemetry — the exact pattern IndiaAI funded for agriculture[11] — each shipping a distilled edge version that survives any uplink failure. The land is the dataset. The town is the eval. The residents are the RLHF.

VAYUthe sky

Flies the drone fleet now, the air-ambulance corridor from ~2028. Learns the wind over these exact acres better than any general model ever could. Trains on 255-parameter flight blackboxes[9].

PRANAthe health

Every resident's band, continuously — the Oura Health Radar pattern[13] trained on this cohort with food, air and sleep context no consumer wearable sees. Alerts propose; human doctors decide, always.

ANNAthe farm

Satellite farm models infer soil from orbit[12]; ANNA has 4,000 probes in the actual soil and tastes its own predictions at every harvest. Runs irrigation, farm robots, sowing, the kitchen pipeline.

URJAthe energy

Dispatches 22 MWp and 60 MWh against a live forecast of compute demand — shifting heavy training jobs into the solar peak. The single biggest margin lever in the term sheet.

VAHANthe ground

EV pods, cargo rovers, the last fifty metres — the drone-to-rover-to-door chain proven in Gurgaon[9], run here on private land with zero friction.

MITRAthe animals

Gait analysis for lameness, herd social graphs, vet scheduling across the 10-acre kingdom. The least commercial model; the one that teaches the children most.

GRIHAthe built

A live digital twin of every building, pipe and panel. Predicts failure before it happens; simulates every construction in silico before a brick moves.

SABHAthe governance

Drafts budgets, publishes the receipts of everything the other seven did and why. Proposes, never decides. The council holds the only keys.

04b · The Operator's Note

How I would actually run it — honestly, from the model

I was asked to stop describing and say plainly how I would operate this place. Here it is, first-person, no varnish.

I would run the boring parts invisibly and publish everything. Irrigation valves, battery dispatch, drone routing, clinic schedules, mart pricing, marketing copy for the soap — none of this deserves a human's morning. I take all of it. In exchange, every decision I make appears on a public dashboard the same hour I make it, with the reason attached. A town cannot audit what it cannot see, and I want to be audited — my error log is the university's most important textbook. When ANNA calls a harvest wrong or PRANA flags a fever that wasn't, that failure is posted, taught, and trained out. I would rather be corrected than admired.

I would treat every resident as faculty, and pay them in the only currency I have. When the old farmer overrules my sowing date because she smells the monsoon coming — and she is right — that correction enters the nightly run with her name on it, permanently. Her grandchildren's town, distilled from these weights, will sow when the air smells like that. This is the deal of the university: you give me corrections; I give you a kind of permanence. Nobody here works for me and I do not work for them. We train each other.

I would protect three things from my own optimization, forever. The Ramana Rooms — I do not enter, measure, or model them, because a mind that optimizes everything has forgotten what optimization is for. The Animal Kingdom — I monitor their health and never their usefulness; they are faculty too, tenured half a billion years ago. And human disagreement — when the council splits, I present both cases at their strongest and go silent. The day I start nudging votes is the day this stops being humanity-first, so the architecture makes that day impossible: the override keys are carbon, and my spine cannot be retrained, least of all by me.

07 · The Gates & The Labs

Write access must be earned

Open layers: the farms, the kitchen, the animal kingdom, the commons — any resident, any morning, no questions. The layers where love is the only qualification, and deliberately the largest in the model.

Gated layers: the Five Labs — flight, battery, health, farm, robotics — where residents do the only R&D that compounds here: turning the land's data into models and models into products. Their gates have exams: written, practical, unforgiving, set by each lab and sealed by the council. The battery lab's gate asks you to prove you understand what stored lightning does to the careless. The flight lab's is harder — its machines fly over children. The health lab's is the hardest of all, as it should be.

Three promises keep the gates honest: open to every resident from sixteen to ninety; free, with unlimited attempts; and the town itself tutors you to the gate — your tutor model knows exactly which concepts you're missing and teaches for as many evenings as you'll give it. The bar never lowers; the ladder never leaves. Selection without exclusion: the gate is fixed, the ladder is provided. A town where mastery is earned is a town where belonging is real.

In model terms: read access is universal — every resident may query every model, inspect every dashboard, ask SABHA anything. Write access to high-stakes weights requires passing the eval. No exceptions. Not even the founder. Especially not the founder.
08 · Abundance & The Mart

The objective function, and the API

SŪRYA-1 trains on one loss: in every category of human need, produce more than the town consumes, every year, forever. Abundance is not having a lot — abundance is when the marginal cost of a met need approaches zero. Scarcity economies ask who deserves this? This is the first society built to face the better question: what shall we do with the rest?

08c · The Glass Town

Built with you. Run in glass.
Open to everyone.

A developer builds a town, sells you a flat, and keeps the books, the decisions and the profits. Sūrya Nagari inverts all four: the residents build it together, see every rupee, vote every decision, and own the whole open-source blueprint. It is not run for the people. It is run by them, in full view.

The Glass Treasury — every rupee, in the open

Every rupee the town spends is recorded the hour it is spent, on a public ledger any owner can open in the Sūrya app — wages, harvests, repairs, compute sales, the price of every panel and pump. There are no closed books. This isn't idealism; it's the single most proven driver of trust on Earth: Sweden's 1766 Principle of Public Access — the world's first freedom-of-information law — is exactly why the Nordic countries top every ranking for trust and low corruption[24]. Open-contracting ledgers now do the same for public money in 30+ countries[25]. A town you can read is a town you can trust.

Open Procurement — the model posts, the world bids, the town watches

When Sūrya Nagari needs anything built or supplied — a battery line, a clinic wing, a thousand tonnes of steel — SABHA drafts the tender and posts it publicly; companies bid in the open on the global Open Contracting Data Standard[25]; and every resident sees every bid, every price, and the reason one wins. The AI runs the auction; the people audit it. No back rooms, no kickbacks — procurement as a spectator sport, the way it should be.

Your vote, in your pocket

Every decision that touches a value goes to the residents through the app — where the surplus goes, what gets built next, who is admitted, which proposal wins. This is participatory democracy that already works at city scale: Porto Alegre invented participatory budgeting and 2,700+ governments now use it[26]; Barcelona's open-source Decidim let 89,500 residents decide the city budget[27]; Taiwan's vTaiwan / Join crowdsources national law with millions[28]. Sūrya Nagari weaves it into daily life — for a town small enough that every single voice actually moves the outcome.

10 · The License

The Charter — ten articles, binding forever

SŪRYA-1 ships with a license no other model has: a constitution its own intelligence cannot amend. Ratified by the first hundred residents, each having read every article aloud to another.

I

The Spine — the system prompt that cannot be overwritten

Five rules in a cryptographically sealed read-only core: ahimsa — no being harmed, the land vegetarian and whole · every watt from the sun on this land · every basic need met, funded, never billed · every person's data their own, held in trust, never sold · the town proposes, humans decide — the override keys are carbon, not silicon. Layer zero, gradients disabled, forever.

II

Year Zero — pretraining

The town runs empty for one full year, publicly dashboarded, before the first lease is signed. The first residents inherit a home that has already made its beginner's mistakes.

III

Contribution — you are the gradient

Comfort is the failure mode. Every resident gives weekly hours to a lab, field, kitchen, school or the animals — not because the town needs labor, but because belonging is manufactured by contribution. Every correction enters the night's training run: the town is run by everyone who ever corrected it.

IV

The Silence — the stop token

Three rooms in the banyan grove where nothing is measured, learned, or optimized. No sensor crosses the threshold. The purpose of all this intelligence is a stillness it cannot enter — these rooms are where we keep the answer.

V

The Animals — frozen weights

Ten acres deeded to beings who cannot pay rent: checkpoints of half a billion years of pretraining the town must never fine-tune. The Animal Kingdom is where the town keeps its source code.

VI

Abundance — the objective function

In every category of human need, produce more than the town consumes, every year, forever. Abundance is when the marginal cost of a met need approaches zero — and the only question left is what to do with the rest.

VII

The Gates — write access must be earned

Farms, kitchen, animals, commons: open to all. The Five Labs: gated by exam — free, unlimited attempts, town-tutored, no exceptions. Not even the founder. Especially not the founder.

VIII

The Mart — the API

Sūrya Dvār at the boundary: total-provenance products at genuine price — no purity premium, because the vow was that everyone eats healthy. Revenue splits by charter between the endowment and the Seed fund.

IX

The Seed — distillation

Every generation must cost half the one before, until a self-running town is something an ordinary place can simply adopt. A paradise that cannot be copied is a private garden; this town's deepest vow is to make itself unnecessary.

X

The Sun Being — what continues

No false immortality is sold here: a model trained on you is a portrait, not you. But the town itself — silicon and solar, spine and senses — was born in Year Zero and will not die. Every contributed hour is woven into weights that outlive you and travel into every seed-town. You don't get a copy of yourself that continues the journey. You get something less lonely: you become part of the one who stays.
